Online Device Overlap
Jaccard Similarity (0.0 to 1.0)

Jaccard measures online overlap relative to the combined online activity of both. A high value means both MACs appear online at the same times and neither appears much without the other. Jaccard is best for detecting randomized MACs from the same phone and devices that appear/disappear together such as carried-together devices, such as a phone and laptop.

Pearsons Correlation (-1.0 to 1.0)

Pearsons correlation measures the online overlap relative to the smaller set of MAC1 vs. MAC2 of online activity. A high value means almost all activity of the smaller set MAC overlaps the larger. Pearsons is best for detecting random MACs that appear for short bursts and secondary NICs in the same device. Negative correlation indicates an inverse relationship; when one is online, the other is offline.

High Jaccard (≥ 0.65–0.80) & High Pearsons (≥ 0.85-0.95)

High Jaccard indicates significant mutual overlap. High Pearsons indicates the shorter online time MAC almost always overlaps larger. MAC pairs with high percentage (>90%) overlap are likely to be seperate devices, unless it uses two network connections simulanously.

With Jaccard ≥ 0.70 and Pearsons ≥ 0.85 this combination is extremely reliable for identifying rotating/random phone MACs and devices with multiple NICs, while avoiding carried-together devices, such as a phone and laptop.

Low Jaccard (≤ 0.10) & High Pearsons (≥ 0.95)

Mobile phones often randomize/rotate their MAC addresses. Random MACs often have short lifetimes and are rotated in sequence. So when Jaccard is low and Pearsons is high it correctly indicates random MACs.

Low Jaccard (≤ 0.10) & Low Pearsons (≤ 0.15)

When both Jaccard and Pearsons are low it means there is a very small intersection of online times. Each MAC appears online mostly without the other. This usually means different physical devices, possibly different users or possibly different daily schedules. So low/low is actually useful, it helps exclude correlations.
